Frequently Asked Questions

Psychologists and psychiatrists both work in the mental health field, but they differ in their training and approach. Psychologists focus on psychotherapy and behavioral interventions, while psychiatrists are medical doctors who can prescribe medication for mental health conditions.

Finding the right therapist involves considering your specific needs, therapy approach preferences, and the therapist's expertise. You can start by researching therapists in your area, reading reviews, and scheduling initial consultations to see if you feel a connection.

Counseling tends to focus on specific issues and practical solutions, often dealing with present challenges. Psychotherapy, on the other hand, delves deeper into the roots of emotional and behavioral patterns, often exploring the past to understand and address current issues.

It's a good idea to consider therapy if you're experiencing persistent emotional distress, difficulties in relationships, or if your daily functioning is impaired. A therapist can help you explore and address these challenges in a supportive and confidential environment.

The duration of therapy varies depending on the individual and the issues being addressed. Short-term therapy may provide immediate relief, while more complex issues may require longer-term commitment. Consistent attendance and active engagement often contribute to positive outcomes.

Yes, therapy is generally confidential. Therapists are bound by ethical and legal obligations to maintain confidentiality. However, there are exceptions, such as situations involving imminent harm to oneself or others, which therapists are obligated to report.
